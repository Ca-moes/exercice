## The Short Answer

**Keep the same plan for 8-12 weeks**, then make small swaps. Don't rotate weekly.

## Why Not Rotate Frequently?

1. **Progressive overload requires consistency.** You can only track whether you're getting stronger if you repeat the same exercises week after week. If you change exercises every week, you never know if you're actually progressing.

2. **Skill acquisition matters.** Bodyweight exercises like pull-ups, pistol squats, and pike push-ups are skills. They improve with practice. Switching too often resets the skill component.

3. **Joint stress from variety is overstated for bodyweight work.** The concern about joint overuse from repeating exercises applies more to heavy barbell training (e.g., bench pressing 100+ kg every week). Bodyweight loads are much lower — the risk is minimal.

## When and What to Swap

After 8-12 weeks, swap **one exercise per workout** — usually an isolation movement, not the core compound. The main movement patterns should stay:

| Workout | Keep (always) | Swap candidate |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| Push | Pike push-ups, push-ups, dips | Deficit push-ups ↔ decline push-ups; overhead extension ↔ skull crushers |
| Pull | Pull-ups, inverted rows | Concentration curls ↔ hammer curls; pull-aparts ↔ face pulls |
| Legs | Bulgarian split squats, B-stance RDL | Sissy squats ↔ step-ups; sliding leg curls ↔ banded leg curls; Cossack squats ↔ lateral lunges |

Note: with the 6-day split, you also have a built-in variety lever — keep the **anchor** movements on both Day 1 and Day 2, and rotate the *day-specific* accessories independently.

## Dealing with Boredom

If boredom hits before 8 weeks:

- **Change the rep scheme** instead of the exercise (e.g., 3x12 → 4x8 with slower tempo)
- **Change the order** — put a different exercise first (when you're freshest)
- **Add a finisher** — a short burnout set at the end (e.g., max push-ups in 60 seconds)
- **Listen to different music/podcasts** — often it's the routine around the workout that's boring, not the exercises themselves

## Long-Term Phases (Optional)

If you want to think in blocks:

| Phase | Duration | Focus | Example change |
|-------|----------|-------|----------------|
| Base | Weeks 1-8 | Learn movements, build consistency | Standard plan as written |
| Progression | Weeks 9-16 | Push reps/tempo harder | Add pauses, slower eccentrics |
| Variation | Weeks 17-20 | Swap 1-2 exercises per workout | Introduce new isolation movements |
| Deload | Week 21 | Half volume, same movements | 2 sets instead of 3, no failure |
| Repeat | Weeks 22+ | New 8-week block with minor changes | — |

## The Bottom Line

Simplicity wins. Six short sessions (PPL ×2), repeated consistently, with gradual progression in reps or difficulty. That's 90% of the game. Save the fancy periodization for when you've been doing this for 6+ months and genuinely feel stale.
